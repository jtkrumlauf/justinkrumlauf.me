Reinforcment Learning

Uploaded on Dec. 10th, 2018

Introduction

In my opinion, reinforcement learning is the most interesting field in artificial intelligence. In concept, reinforcement learning is fairly simple. It works on a reward system, rewarding the model when it does something correct and punishing it when it does something wrong. Reinforcement learning is used to find the ideal solution, maximizing the reward the model is receiving. Imagine the following example: you are standing in front of a massive hedge maze. Every time you reach a dead end, you are forced to start again. Imagine that everytime you make a correct turn, you receive $10, but every time you make a wrong turn, you loose $20. As a result, you will learn the optimal way to traverse the maze while maximizing the money you gain. In the following post, I will detail how reinforcement learning algorithms are used and a specific form of reinforcement learning: Q-Learning.

Q Learning

Q-Learning finds an optimal solution in the sense that it maximizes the total reward over n successive steps, starting from the current state. Q-Learning can find the optimal actions based on any given finite Markov Decision Process, given infinite exploration time. In other words, Q-Learning is theoretically able to find the solution to any finite amount of choices. It’s goal is to maximize its total (future) reward. There are many applications of Q-Learning, my favorite being the deep Q Learning methods used in Mario-AI. The algorithm uses raw pixel sequences and saves them into memory and trains on them later, where “training” means to accurately predict the expected action-reward values based on collected memories. When the memory starts to get full (default size of 250k entries), new entries start to replace older ones. For training data, batches, examples are chosen randomly and rewards of memories are re-estimated based on what the network has learned so far. After tons of math, dropout, ReLu, the model is finally able to play the game in the most optimized way possible. You can visit Aleju’s GitHub repository (https://github.com/aleju/mario-ai) to learn more about his implementation.

Conclusion

Reinforcement Learning is being used by cutting edge researchers to help developed artificial general intelligence (AGI). OpenAI are at the forefront of this development (their research can be found here: https://openai.com/research/#publications) and are using various methods of reinforcement learning to train advanced models. Overall, there are many applications of Reinforcement Learning are seemingly infinite and will shape AI in the near-future.

Machine Learning Basics

Uploaded on Dec. 11th, 2018

Supervised Learning

Supervised learning is typically used for classification; when we want to map input to outputs or regression. The goal is to find specific relationships between different input data in order to produce a correct output. However, the only way for model to produce a ‘correct’ is due to the training data. In the real world, training data is often ‘noisy’, or incorrect, which will reduce the effectiveness of your model. Ultimately, supervised learning is used when you know what the output should be, giving explicit labels to data. There are many algorithms that incorporate aspects of supervised learning, however the most common are regression and support vector machines (SVMs). These algorithms are used in aspects of speech recognition, spam detection, pattern recognition and countless others.

Unsupervised Learning

Unlike supervised learning, unsupervised learning uses test data that has not been classified, labeled or otherwise categorized. Rather than classifying information and responding to feedback, unsupervised learning reacts based on presence or absence of data. Consider the following analogy: Imagine putting a young child in a room with a box that has a square hole cut in the top, then giving him different shaped blocks (a triangle, circle, hexagon, square, etc.). You leave the child in the room for an indeterminate amount of time and when you return, you give the child a new set of blocks. You hope the child is able to correctly select what block fits in the top of the box and which blocks don’t. In this example, the child represents the model and blocks initial blocks represent the training data. You hope the child will be able to solve the puzzle given a new set of blocks, but don’t know for sure, which is similar to unsupervised learning. Unsupervised learning is often used in conjunction with neural networks, which will be discussed in further detail later. Some common algorithms that use unsupervised learning are k-means and clustering.

Conclusion

In order to understand more complex concepts in machine learning and artificial intelligence, it is imperative that one knows the difference between supervised and unsupervised learning. It is important to remember that in supervised learning, you are using labeled and classified data sets. While in unsupervised learning you use more of a ‘hope for the best’ approach. Ultimately, both methods are integral to a deep understanding of machine learning and AI as a whole.

Neural Networks

Uploaded on Dec. 11th, 2018

Introduction

Neural networks: one of the hallmarks of artificial intelligence and machine learning. While they are arguably one of the most well known ‘buzzwords’ relating to AI, they are often poorly understood. While I have a pretty high-level understanding of some neural networks, I hope to explain some basic functions of them in an easy to understand way. I will detail Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). Many of these networks are too complex to go into great detail, this will just be a basic overview of the aforementioned networks. While I try to use high level terminology to explain the network’s workings, some words/theories you will not recognize. I highly recommend you look them up and get a basic understanding for what it is before continuing.

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)

A very new form of neural network, the Generative Adversarial Network was created Ian Goodfellow in 2014. Using unsupervised learning, GANs consist of two contesting neural networks in a zero-sum game framework. Using this technique, the network can generate photographs that can have many realistic qualities depending on the training set. GANs are commonly used today for superimposing someone’s face on another's, making it seem like a person is saying something they actually aren't. Below, there is an example of a GAN generating human faces give other training data. In this example, it takes (what I assume) is thousands of photos of training data to output an image of a woman with glasses. Given what the network knows about the face of a man with glasses, a man without glasses and a woman without glasses, the network is able to create a new photo of a woman wearing glasses.

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)

Recurrent Neural Networks are among the newest form of neural network to be developed. The best way to think of a RNN is to imagine the output being fed back into the input along a new set of input values, almost like a loop. Despite this, they have the most complicated architecture of all existing networks. Due to this, they are extremely difficult to train and use properly. However, with recent advancements in Hessian-free optimization (some crazy mathematical model combining multidimensional Newton’s Method, gradient descent, and conjugate gradient. So new, there is no wikipedia page) it has made it more possible to successfully train and apply RNNs to complex problems. Netflix uses a combination of a CNN and RNN to recommend you things to watch based on previous viewings. It even chooses the thumbnail of the show based on previous interactions. Researchers at the University of Toronto have developed with largest known RNN (as of 2011) to date. Keep an eye on their research as they progress, it will be invaluable as the use of RNNs expands in the future.

Conclusion

I hope this clarifies some of your questions about some neural networks. While I chose these three (CNNs, GANs, RNNs), there are countless other forms of networks that are being researched. Hopefully this gives you enough knowledge to further understand the inner workings of neural networks and how they are used in the real world.
